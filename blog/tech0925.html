Mixed Signals

The rise and fall of digital literacy

Chrissy Cuskley, November 2024

How curricular reforms gutted basic computer skills, and why it won't get better anytime soon.

It's been a rough few years for higher education in the UK. Widespread and almost constant industrial action, pivots between online and in person teaching, and financial woes for both students and institutions. But among these looming systemic challenges lies a more pernicious one: a precipitous drop in knowledge and skills related to technology among university students, especially in the social sciences and humanities.

I teach programming, data analysis, and experimental methods in a school that is dominated by English literature. It is not something my students are necessarily expecting - many of them abandoned "hard" STEM subjects like mathematics and computing at the first opportunity, probably in their early teens. 20 years ago, many linguistics departments may have been more theoretical than anything else. Now, any competitive linguistics programme should provide students a certain level of data literacy, or at least offer them the opportunity for it. A linguistics graduate should be able to engage with quantitative methods at a fairly high level, at the very least as consumers, if not as producers, of primary research.

Nonetheless, when students sign up for my module, they are often surprised by the content. It is titled "Experimental Methods in Linguistics", and there is clear detail in the description that they will be learning and using the programming language R. In the first year I taught this module, a student was nearly in tears and informed me "they did not study English to do maths". Over the years, multiple students have asked if they need to “know how to use a computer”. When I ask for a novel experimental design with details about a recruitment strategy, I've had multiple students suggest they can “post” an email to everyone in the university, indicating they see this as some kind of closed social network more like Facebook and less like a descendant of snail mail. When I tell students they will need to use a desktop computer to complete their assignments, I often get dismissive eyerolls. Over the course of a few years, I've done a little typing test with my students just to see where they are - typing speeds below 30 words per minute with less than 60% accuracy are not unusual (:what is?)

Some of this is purely the widening gap between me and my students: I am an 80s baby; my parents met when they were both programmers in the financial sector. There were computers in my house before there were computers in many houses, but my education in the US was also fundamentally different. I was drilled in touch typing with the now problematic Mavis Beacon[NUTSHELL] from about the age of 8, and I can now stare out a window while typing at around 80-90 words per minute with little loss in accuracy [NUTSHELL: I consider this a superpower]. I know my way around a computer, but I don't really remember being taught how to use one any more than I remember learning how to use a fork. It is something I just grew up with in the ambient atmosphere, and I absorbed a lot. I never really studied computers; I originally applied to university to do studio art and ended up in Psychology. It wasn't until years after my undergraduate, when I had to build my own interfaces for behavioural expeirments during my PhD in linguistics, that I began to program[NUTSHELL (at the time, R was barely a thing and Python wasn't either)]. I was able to Google may way into programming in my mid-twenties in part because I hadn't been bottlenecked into a particular subject in my teens in the way that students are in the UK (and in England in particular). No one was encouraging a girl who liked to paint to become a computer scientist, but no one was saying she couldn't mess around with code either

In contrast, many of my students fled from "difficult" subjects in the sciences at the first opportunity. In England and Wales, that opportunity comes along when students are about 14-16, an age group well-known for their sound decision making, penetrating foresight, and penchant for sticking with difficult things even if they may be slightly unpleasant in the moment. I'm not picking on a handful of students that don't get something: this is a critical mass indicative of a more widespread problem. I don't work at some impoverished institution with a reputation for under-prepared undergrads: I work at a Russel Group University with rigorous entry requirements [NUTSHELL: this is sort of the equivalent of a US R1, although dumber because some men invented it ina hotel in 1992]. My university also has a computer science programme: as far as I can gather, lecturers there do not encounter the same worrying anecdotes surrounding digital literacy that I do. What is going on?
